    Karl SchultzCommunity TA· a day ago 
    You should first decide if the data really does have outliers.  For the first plot, you're going to form totals using all the data and the sums are going to easily reach into the millions of tons.  How does your outlier contribute to this total?  Is it still unreasonable?

    You also probably have to consider the reliability of the data.  Do you have any real reason to suspect that the outliers are really bad readings?  Is there anything in the documentation that suggests it could be bad?  Conversely, is there any statement in the documentation saying that the data has been scrubbed and deemed correct?  Have other people looked at it and pointed out any problems and/or got them fixed?

    Could it be that emissions were just really bad for a few readings?  Maybe somebody parked a car with a broken exhaust system next to a sensor one day.  A fluke, yes, but it still contributes to emissions.

    If a sensor is truly broken, it might read 0 or some max value.

    If you really want to remove data from the analysis, it is important that you document that you did so, provide the details, and explain why you did it.  You would do this in comments in your R script, and/or in any required docs for the project.  The need to document what you did is probably actually more important than the basis you used to choose to remove data.

    As for the code, once you have decided on an upper bound,

    data <- data[data$column <= bound]

    My take:  I decided not to remove any outliers.   There are other factors that affect a valid year-by-year comparison.  One is that there are different numbers of observations for each year.  For example, if one year has 1000's of more observations than another year, that difference could easily cancel out the effect of an outlier.  

    I think that the point of the exercise is to quickly do Exploratory Data Analysis on this data that we're assuming is correct enough for the purposes of this exercise.  We do this by making the plots and looking for general trends.  A more serious analysis would probably perform steps like investigating outliers and keeping the set of sensors constant over the study period.

    Karl SchultzCommunity TA· 20 hours ago 
    This is a good question and is probably going to stimulate a lot of discussion, because there is no clear answer.  You can basically decide "what to grep for" yourself and then just go with that.  And, unfortunately, the range of reasonable choices here will result in a range of reasonable plots, so not all plots are going to look the same.

    Again, it is best to document, via comments in the code, what you chose to search for and why.  This assignment is really more about getting the plot on the screen, rather then making the perfect choice of what data to plot.

    I chose a fairly simple "grep string", very similar to yours, and just went with it ("vehicle" from EI.Sector, ignoring case).  I chose to spend more time on learning the various plotting features, rather than tweaking the filter.  

    You might watch the forums and see what other people decide to do.  Once you get your plots right, you can then fiddle with the filter and see how the plots change.  Your code would likely work for most variations of your filter.
